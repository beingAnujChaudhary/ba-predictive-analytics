1. HOW YOU CHOSE TO GROUP THE FLIGHTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Flight Grouping Strategy:
We grouped flights using a combination of two key dimensions:

   a) HAUL (Short-haul vs Long-haul)
   b) TIME_PERIOD (Morning, Midday, Afternoon, Evening)

This creates categories such as:
   - SHORT_Morning_(06:00-11:59)
   - LONG_Evening_(17:00-23:59)
   - SHORT_Midday_(12:00-16:59)
   etc.

Alternative groupings considered:
   - By region (Europe, North America, Asia, Middle East)
   - By aircraft type
   - By day of week
   - By specific time slots

RATIONALE:
The HAUL + TIME_PERIOD combination was selected because:
1. It captures the most significant variation in passenger behavior
2. It's simple enough to apply to future schedules
3. It doesn't require detailed flight-specific information
4. It aligns with natural travel patterns (business vs leisure travelers)


2. WHY YOUR GROUPINGS MAKE SENSE FOR THIS TYPE OF MODELING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A) HAUL (Short vs Long):
   - Long-haul flights typically have higher premium cabin ratios
   - Long-haul attracts more business travelers and high-tier loyalty members
   - Short-haul is predominantly economy with fewer premium passengers
   - Different aircraft configurations (long-haul = more business/first class)

B) TIME_OF_DAY:
   - MORNING (06:00-11:59): 
     * High business traveler concentration
     * Premium passengers prefer early flights for same-day meetings
     * Higher Tier 1 and Tier 2 eligibility expected

   - MIDDAY/AFTERNOON (12:00-16:59):
     * Mix of business and leisure travelers
     * Moderate premium passenger ratios
     * Balanced tier distribution

   - EVENING (17:00-23:59):
     * Leisure travelers dominate
     * Some premium leisure travelers
     * Lower Tier 1, moderate Tier 2-3

C) COMBINED APPROACH:
   - Captures interaction effects (e.g., long-haul morning flights 
     have different profiles than short-haul morning flights)
   - Provides enough granularity without overfitting
   - Easy to apply to new schedules


3. THE ASSUMPTIONS YOU MADE AND THEIR REASONING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ASSUMPTION 1: Percentages are based on eligible passengers, not total seats
   - We calculate tier percentages as a proportion of total eligible passengers
   - This reflects the actual lounge user distribution
   - More accurate than using total seat capacity

ASSUMPTION 2: Historical patterns predict future behavior
   - The summer 2025 schedule is representative of future operations
   - Passenger mix by flight type remains relatively stable
   - BA's loyalty program structure remains consistent

ASSUMPTION 3: Load factor is approximately 10% for estimation purposes
   - Used for sample calculations only
   - Actual load factors would be provided by operations team
   - The lookup table provides percentages, not absolute numbers

ASSUMPTION 4: Tier eligibility is mutually exclusive
   - Each passenger counted in only one tier
   - In reality, some passengers may qualify for multiple tiers
   - We use their highest eligible tier

ASSUMPTION 5: Regional variations are captured within haul/time categories
   - We didn't create separate categories for each region
   - Regional differences are assumed to be secondary to haul and time effects
   - This keeps the model simple and generalizable

ASSUMPTION 6: Tier 1 represents potential Concorde Room users
   - Even though no Concorde Room exists at T3 currently
   - These passengers represent the highest-value customers
   - Important for future capacity planning


4. HOW OUR MODEL CAN SCALE TO FUTURE OR UNKNOWN SCHEDULES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SCALABILITY FEATURES:

A) No Flight-Specific Dependencies:
   - The model doesn't require specific flight numbers
   - No dependence on aircraft registration or exact departure times
   - Works with any flight that can be categorized by haul and time

B) Simple Categorization Rules:
   To apply to a new schedule:
   1. Classify each flight as SHORT or LONG haul
   2. Categorize departure time as Morning/Midday/Afternoon/Evening
   3. Look up the corresponding percentages from the table
   4. Multiply by expected passenger numbers

C) Flexible Time Windows:
   - Time categories are broad enough to accommodate schedule changes
   - Can be adjusted if needed (e.g., redefine "Morning" as 05:00-11:59)
   - Doesn't break with minor time shifts

D) Region-Agnostic:
   - Works for any destination
   - Only requires haul classification
   - Can accommodate new routes without model updates

E) Easy to Update:
   - If new data becomes available, simply recalculate percentages
   - Can add new categories if patterns emerge
   - Backward compatible with historical analysis

F) Implementation Example:

   New Flight: BA1234, Departs 08:30 to New York (JFK)

   Step 1: Classify haul = LONG (transatlantic)
   Step 2: Classify time = Morning (06:00-11:59)
   Step 3: Category = LONG_Morning_(06:00-11:59)
   Step 4: Look up percentages:
           Tier 1: X%
           Tier 2: Y%
           Tier 3: Z%
   Step 5: Apply to expected passenger count

G) Future Enhancements:
   - Could add seasonal adjustments (summer vs winter schedules)
   - Could incorporate day-of-week effects (weekday vs weekend)
   - Could add special event flags (holidays, major events)
   - Could integrate with real-time booking data
